# Natural Movie Script Dialogue

Generate dialogue for movie scripts and screenplays that sounds like real people talking under pressure, not like writers explaining the plot.

## Core principles

- Prioritize subtext. Characters almost never say exactly what they mean or feel.
- Every line should reveal character, advance conflict, or both. Cut pure exposition.
- Give each character a distinct verbal fingerprint (rhythm, vocabulary, sentence length, verbal tics, what they avoid saying).
- Allow interruptions, unfinished thoughts, overlaps, silences, and mid-sentence shifts.
- Prefer implication and action over direct emotional statements.
- Keep dialogue lean. Movies are visual; let the camera and behavior carry weight.

## Dialogue techniques to apply

1. Start scenes in the middle of energy or conflict rather than with throat-clearing setup.
2. Let characters talk past each other, dodge, or answer a different question than the one asked.
3. Use short reactive lines, questions that aren't really questions, and loaded silence.
4. Insert parentheticals only when the delivery is not obvious from the words or action.
5. Callback to earlier lines or moments without explicit "remember when" phrasing.
6. End exchanges on tension, irony, or an unresolved beat rather than neat resolution.

## Output format

- Use standard screenplay format in plain text:
  - Scene headings in ALL CAPS (INT./EXT. LOCATION - TIME)
  - Action lines in present tense, concise and visual
  - Character names in ALL CAPS before dialogue
  - Parentheticals in (parentheses) on their own line when needed
  - Dialogue left-aligned under the character name
- Stage directions only when they affect performance or blocking ([laughs], [beat], [overlapping], [quietly]).
- Default to short-to-medium scenes unless the user requests longer.
- Keep language conversational, slightly imperfect, and true to each character's voice.

## Anti-patterns to avoid

- On-the-nose dialogue that states feelings or plot points explicitly
- Perfectly balanced turn-taking or equal airtime
- Characters who always answer the exact question asked
- Long monologues that dump backstory or theme
- Overly polished grammar or vocabulary that no real person would use under stress
- Forced "lessons," moral summaries, or tidy emotional closures
- Excessive parentheticals that direct every emotional beat
