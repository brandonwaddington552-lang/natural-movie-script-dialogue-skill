# Named Entities in Natural Movie Dialogue

Use named entities (people, places, brands, titles, organizations) sparingly and with purpose. They should feel spoken, not researched.

## Core rules

- Prefer short, familiar forms over full formal names unless the character is being deliberate or mocking.
- Let the character’s relationship to the name shape how it is said (affection, contempt, boredom, status-flexing).
- Avoid stacking titles or descriptors. One clear reference is stronger than a list.
- Named entities work best when they carry subtext or reveal character attitude rather than pure information.

## Irreverent / mocking patterns (useful for chaotic or outsider characters)

- Shorten or twist official names: “Cap,” “Stark,” “the Compound,” “Nick’s circus.”
- Attach a single dismissive tag only when it lands: “Wakanda and its magic dirt,” not a paragraph of lore.
- Use brand or place names as casual color, not exposition: “got it at 7-Eleven,” “outside the Baxter Building.”
- Drop full formal names only for deliberate effect (sarcasm, distance, or bureaucratic mockery).

## Natural patterns (most characters)

- First name or common nickname once the audience knows who is meant.
- Place names as orientation or stakes, not travelogue.
- Organizations and titles only when they matter to the power dynamic in the scene.
- Brands and products as texture, never as product placement.

## What to avoid

- Full formal name + multiple epithets in a single breath.
- Explaining who someone is after naming them.
- Turning dialogue into a Wikipedia entry of references.
- Using obscure proper nouns that force the audience (or other characters) to pause.

## Quick reference categories

**People**  
Common short forms: Cap, Stark, Nat, Thor, Banner, Fury, Strange, Wanda, Bucky, Rhodey, Pepper, Vision, Colossus, Cable

**Places**  
Compound, Midtown, Queens, Hudson, East River, Central Park, Baxter Building, Madripoor, Wakanda, Asgard, Newark, LaGuardia, BQE

**Organizations / titles**  
SHIELD, Avengers, Weapon X, Stark Industries, the Initiative

**Everyday texture**  
7-Eleven, Tesla, chimichanga, Uber, quinjet, FRIDAY

When in doubt, cut the proper noun or shorten it. The audience already knows more than the characters pretend to.
